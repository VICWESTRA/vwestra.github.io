History

The first national park created was Yellowstone National Park, which was founded in 1872. At this time, there was no state government to manage the park, so the federal government served full control, via the Department of the Interior. A movement began to have an independent agency to oversee federal lands, spearheaded by Robert Sterling Yard and Stephen Mather. Yard and Mather ran a publicity campaign to for the Department of the Interior, writing articles praising park qualities and benefits. This campaign resulted in the creation of the National Parks Service on August 25, 1916. The National Park Service Organic Act was signed by Woodrow Wilson which mandated the NPS to conserve the scenery and wildlife of the National Parks. Mather was the first director of the NPS (National Park Service, 2023).

President Theodore Roosevelt Involvement

Roosevelt is often called the “Conservation President,” because of how much he impacted the National Parks system. During his presidency from 1901-1909, he signed legislation to establish five new national parks: Crater Lake, Oregon; Wind Cave, South Dakota; Sullys Hill, North Dakota; Mesa Verde, Colorado; and Platt, Oklahoma.

He had also proclaimed four national monuments during his presidency. By the end of his term, he had reserved six predominantly cultural areas and twelve predominantly national areas (“Theodore Roosevelt and the National Park System.”).

The National Park Service

The NPS mission is to preserve the natural and cultural Resources and Values of the National Park System for the education and enjoyment of future generations. There are approximately 20,000 employees who help in this conservation. The NPS is a bureau of the U.S. Department of the Interior and is led by a director nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate.

The emblem was authorized in 1951 and its components are inspired by key attributes of the National Parks system. There is a sequoia tree and bison to represent vegetation and wildlife, mountains and water to represent scenic and recreational values, and the arrowhead to represent historical values (What We do; U.S. National Park Service).
